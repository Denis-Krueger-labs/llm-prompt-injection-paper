\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:fazit}

The effectiveness of learned instruction--data separation depends on the attack
and evaluation setting. In their original evaluations, StruQ and SecAlign
substantially reduce success against the tested handcrafted attacks. Standard
optimisation-based attacks produce mixed results, with the final SecAlign study
showing a strong within-study improvement over StruQ. Broader and
defence-adaptive white-box evaluations nevertheless find successful bypasses of
both approaches. Whether those audit results transfer to closed, dynamically
configured production systems remains an empirical question.

The evidence therefore supports mitigation, not complete prevention. StruQ and
SecAlign learn to prioritise a trusted channel, but the decision remains part of
probabilistic model behaviour. The reviewed bypasses show that these defences do
not establish a demonstrated deterministic or independently enforced security
boundary under the evaluated conditions. They do not prove that every present
or future model-level defence is impossible.

Secure deployment must consequently constrain model failures at the system
level. Provenance should be preserved, tools and credentials should follow least
privilege, security-relevant parameters should be checked outside the model, and
high-impact actions should pass an independent policy or approval step.
Sandboxing, protected monitoring, realistic adversarial testing, and incident
response provide containment, detection, and recovery rather than substituting
for access control.

Architectural approaches can provide stronger, externally enforceable
guarantees only under explicit assumptions about labels, policies, trusted
interpreters, tool wrappers, and the trusted computing base. The resulting
defence-in-depth conclusion is therefore deliberately conditional: learned
separation is useful risk reduction, while system-level controls limit the
consequences when that behavioural protection fails.
