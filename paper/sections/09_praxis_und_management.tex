\section{Practical Implications and Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:praxis}

\subsection{Prompt Injection as a System-Level Risk}

Modern LLM deployments rarely operate in isolation. Instead, they are typically
embedded within larger systems that may include retrieval pipelines, external
APIs, memory modules, and tool execution environments. Consequently, a
successful prompt injection does not merely influence the generated response,
but can propagate into other components of the surrounding application.

The severity of a successful prompt injection therefore depends primarily on
the capabilities granted to the surrounding system rather than the language
model alone.

For example, a read-only document summarisation system presents a much lower
risk than an autonomous email assistant capable of sending messages or
interacting with external services. 

As AgentDojo demonstrates, the payoff from a successful injection scales with
the capabilities available to the model~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo}. Real-world incidents such as the
Slack AI attack illustrate this point further~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{Defence in Depth}

Because prompt injection cannot currently be eliminated entirely, practical
deployments must assume that individual defensive layers will occasionally
fail. Consequently, secure LLM systems should be designed according to the
principle of defence in depth, limiting the impact of successful injections
rather than relying on any single protective mechanism.

In practice, this means combining model-level defences with restricted tool
permissions, sandboxed execution environments, monitoring, and human
confirmation requirements for irreversible actions. These controls should
ideally be formalised through organisational AI governance policies rather
than implemented in an ad hoc manner by individual application teams.

No individual layer is sufficient on its own, but together they increase the
difficulty of successful attacks and limit the blast radius if one occurs.
This mirrors the established defence-in-depth principle from traditional
software security~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{Deployment Trade-Offs}

No currently available approach offers an ideal combination of security,
usability, and ease of deployment. Architectural approaches based on
information flow control generally offer stronger guarantees under their
respective assumptions, but require substantial redesign of existing
application pipelines~\parencite{wu2024systemlevel, zhong2025rtbas}.

Fine-tuning approaches are easier to integrate but do not hold up well against
adaptive attackers~\parencite{yang2025checkpointgcg}. This makes such
approaches attractive for organisations seeking rapid deployment, but the
resulting convenience comes at the cost of weaker security guarantees compared
to more intrusive architectural solutions.

Detection systems add minimal operational overhead but both miss attacks and
flag legitimate inputs~\parencite{jia2025critical}.

In commercial environments, these trade-offs are harder to navigate. Pressure
to deploy quickly and maintain ease of use often conflicts with conservative
security design. As LLM systems take on more sensitive operations and broader
tool access, the cost of underestimating prompt injection risks increases
accordingly.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Beyond the limitations of current approaches, several broader challenges
remain open for both research and practical deployment.

Stronger architectural isolation --- enforcing trust boundaries through system
design rather than training --- is a more fundamental approach than continued
fine-tuning improvements~\parencite{wu2024systemlevel, debenedetti2025defeating}.

Evaluation methodology also needs to improve: adaptive attackers should be the
baseline rather than an edge case, and utility costs need to be measured
consistently~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo, fspib2025, jia2025critical}.

Emerging areas such as multimodal prompt injection and increasingly autonomous
agent systems will likely introduce additional challenges, requiring both new
benchmark suites and stronger forms of architectural protection.

Finally, increased model capability does not automatically improve security ---
a more capable model that is successfully injected can also do more
damage~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo}.

Prompt injection therefore remains an open problem. Current defences reduce
risk, but they do not eliminate the underlying vulnerability.
