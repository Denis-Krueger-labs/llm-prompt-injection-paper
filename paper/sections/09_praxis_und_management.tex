\section{Practical Implications and Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:praxis}

\subsection{Prompt Injection as a System-Level Risk}

A key conclusion from the research surveyed in this paper is that prompt
injection cannot be adequately addressed by treating it as a model-level
problem. In practical deployments, the LLM is one component within a larger
system that may include retrieval pipelines, external APIs, memory modules, and
tool execution environments. A successful injection does not just produce a
problematic output --- it can propagate into all of those components.

This shifts the relevant question. Rather than asking whether the model can be
manipulated, it is more useful to ask what an attacker can accomplish once
manipulation occurs. As AgentDojo demonstrates, the payoff from a successful
injection scales with the capabilities of the surrounding
system~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo}. Real-world incidents such as the
Slack AI attack illustrate that these consequences extend beyond the model
itself~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{Defence in Depth}

Since no single mechanism provides reliable protection, current research
increasingly recommends a layered approach. The objective is not to prevent
every injection, but to ensure that a successful one cannot escalate into a
critical incident.

In practice, this means combining model-level defences with restricted tool
permissions, sandboxed execution environments, monitoring, and human
confirmation requirements for irreversible actions. No individual layer is
sufficient on its own, but together they raise the cost of a successful attack
and limit the blast radius if one occurs. This mirrors the established
defence-in-depth principle from traditional software
security~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{Deployment Trade-Offs}

No currently available approach offers an ideal combination of security,
usability, and ease of deployment. Architectural approaches based on
information flow control provide stronger guarantees but require significant
changes to existing systems~\parencite{wu2024systemlevel, zhong2025rtbas}.
Fine-tuning approaches are easier to integrate but do not hold up well against
adaptive attackers~\parencite{yang2025checkpointgcg}. Detection systems add
minimal operational overhead but both miss attacks and flag legitimate
inputs~\parencite{jia2025critical}.

In commercial environments, these trade-offs are harder to navigate. Pressure
to deploy quickly and maintain ease of use often conflicts with conservative
security design. As LLM systems take on more sensitive operations and broader
tool access, the cost of underestimating prompt injection risks increases
accordingly.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several directions emerge from the limitations identified in this paper.
Stronger architectural isolation --- enforcing trust boundaries through system
design rather than training --- is a more fundamental approach than continued
fine-tuning improvements~\parencite{wu2024systemlevel, debenedetti2025defeating}.
Evaluation methodology also needs to improve: adaptive attackers should be the
baseline, not an edge case, and utility costs need to be measured
consistently~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo, fspib2025, jia2025critical}.
Finally, increased model capability does not automatically improve security ---
a more capable model that is successfully injected can also do more
damage~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo}.

Prompt injection is not a solved problem. Current defences reduce the risk but
do not eliminate the underlying vulnerability.
