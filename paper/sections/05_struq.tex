\section{StruQ}
\label{sec:struq}

\subsection{Structured Queries}

This section examines concrete defence mechanisms proposed to mitigate prompt
injection attacks. One of the earliest approaches is StruQ, introduced by
\textcite{chen2025struq}.

The vulnerability exploited by prompt injection stems primarily from the
model's inability to distinguish between trusted instructions and untrusted
data. StruQ addresses this problem by introducing structured queries, which
explicitly separate instructions and user data into distinct channels
\parencite{chen2025struq}.

Another relevant approach is SecAlign, which adopts a different fine-tuning
strategy. Rather than relying on explicit separation through delimiter tokens,
SecAlign trains the model to distinguish between desirable and undesirable
responses to prompt-injected inputs~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{StruQ's Architecture}

StruQ implements this separation through a secure front-end that formats the
input using reserved delimiter tokens, namely [INST] for instructions, [INPT]
for data, and [RESP] for model responses. To prevent so-called completion
attacks, instances of these tokens are recursively removed from user-provided
content~\parencite{chen2025struq}.

Because conventional LLMs are generally trained to follow instructions
regardless of their position within the context window, StruQ introduces a
fine-tuning strategy called structured instruction tuning. Starting from a
base model, the approach employs a specially augmented dataset designed to
discourage the model from following instructions embedded in the data
channel~\parencite{chen2025struq}.


\subsection{StruQ Impact and Limitations}

Experimental results show that StruQ reduces the success rate of heuristic
attacks to below 2\%~\parencite{chen2025struq}. However, the approach does not
provide complete protection. More advanced attacks remain effective even
against defended models.

One example is the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) attack, which generates
adversarial token sequences designed to increase the likelihood that the model
follows the injected instructions~\parencite{zou2023universal}. While StruQ
reduces GCG attack success rates compared to undefended baselines, which suffer
ASRs above 84,\%, the attack still succeeds in 40,\%--60,\% of cases on
several evaluated models, making it high enough to remain problematic in
practical deployments~\parencite{chen2025struq, chen2025secalign}.

Furthermore, StruQ is primarily suited for structured and programmatic
applications. Its reliance on explicitly separated channels limits its
applicability in open-ended conversational systems
~\parencite{chen2025struq}.

\subsection{SecAlign}

SecAlign adopts a different fine-tuning strategy while pursuing the same goal
of improving robustness against prompt injection. Whereas StruQ trains the
model to respect explicit boundaries between instructions and data, SecAlign
uses datasets consisting of prompt-injected inputs together with desirable and
undesirable responses~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

Through preference optimisation, the model is encouraged to maximise the
probability of secure responses while suppressing outputs triggered by
injected instructions. This increases the separation between desirable and
undesirable behaviours, leading to improved robustness compared to standard
fine-tuning~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{SecAlign Impact}

Compared to StruQ, SecAlign achieves substantially improved robustness against
optimisation-based attacks such as GCG. On instruction-tuned models, the
maximum optimisation-based attack success rate drops from 27--45,\% under
StruQ to below 10,\% under SecAlign, a reduction by a factor of more than
four across all evaluated models~\parencite{chen2025secalign}. A further
advantage of SecAlign is that it does not require users to explicitly label
instructions and data, making it easier to integrate into existing
applications~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\medskip

Both StruQ and SecAlign demonstrate that fine-tuning can significantly improve
robustness against prompt injection attacks. In particular, they show that
explicitly modelling the distinction between instructions and data increases
the effort required for successful attacks and reduces the effectiveness of
many heuristic techniques.

However, neither approach provides complete protection. StruQ remains primarily
suitable for structured applications and is vulnerable to optimisation-based
attacks, while SecAlign, despite its improved robustness, can still be bypassed
by adaptive adversaries. Consequently, these approaches should be viewed as
important improvements rather than definitive solutions.
