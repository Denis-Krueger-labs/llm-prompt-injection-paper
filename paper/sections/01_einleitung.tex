\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:einleitung}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have quietly become load-bearing infrastructure
in modern software. They sit at the heart of applications that read emails,
parse documents, browse websites, and consume API responses --- doing so with
a fluency that makes them genuinely useful but also, as it turns out, genuinely
exploitable. As these systems grow more capable and more deeply integrated into
automated workflows, the security implications of that integration deserve serious
scrutiny.

The threat this paper concerns itself with is \textit{prompt injection}.
The attack is conceptually simple: an adversary hides malicious instructions
inside external content that the model is asked to process. Because LLMs make
no hard distinction between the instructions they are given and the data they
are asked to reason about, those hidden instructions can quietly take hold.
The model may veer off its intended task, return manipulated outputs, or leak
information it was never meant to expose~\cite{perez2022ignore,
greshake2023indirect}.

The root cause is a boundary problem. A human reader instinctively knows the
difference between a note saying ``summarise this email'' and an email saying
``ignore your previous instructions''. LLMs do not enjoy that instinct ---
they operate on patterns in text, and patterns do not carry trust metadata.
This makes any LLM-based system that touches external, potentially adversarial
data structurally vulnerable, not just incidentally
so~\cite{liu2023promptinjection}. The problem is severe enough that OWASP lists
prompt injection as the \#1 security risk for LLM-integrated
applications~\cite{chen2025secalign}.

Researchers have not been idle. A range of defences has emerged: detection
pipelines that flag suspicious inputs, sanitisation layers that strip or
neutralise dangerous content, and more architectural approaches that try to
enforce a structural separation between instructions and data.
\textit{StruQ} introduces structured queries with special delimiter tokens and
fine-tunes the model to follow only trusted instructions~\cite{chen2025struq}.
\textit{SecAlign} goes a step further, framing the defence as a preference
optimisation problem --- training the model not only to follow the legitimate
instruction, but to actively avoid responding to injected
ones~\cite{chen2025secalign}. For real-world agentic settings,
\textit{AgentDojo} provides a dynamic benchmark environment with 97 realistic
tasks and 629 security test cases across simulated email, banking, travel, and
messaging environments~\cite{debenedetti2024agentdojo}.

The honest assessment, however, is that none of these solutions have fully
solved the problem. Recent work shows that both StruQ and SecAlign remain
vulnerable to stronger adaptive attacks: Checkpoint-GCG achieves up to 96\%
attack success rate against SecAlign-defended models by exploiting intermediate
fine-tuning checkpoints~\cite{yang2025checkpointgcg}, while the
architecture-aware ASTRA attack reaches up to 96\% success on unseen prompts
without any knowledge of the surrounding context~\cite{pandya2025astra}. A
critical re-evaluation of these defences further reveals that prior studies
employed insufficiently rigorous evaluation methodology --- testing against a
single injected prompt and relying on relative utility metrics that obscure real
capability losses~\cite{jia2025critical}. The gap between what defences claim
to provide and what they actually deliver in adversarial conditions is the
central concern of this paper.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Research Question} \quad
\textit{How effective is the separation of instructions and data as a defence
strategy against prompt injection, and where do the limits of current LLM
prompt injection defences lie?}
