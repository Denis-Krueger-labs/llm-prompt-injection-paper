\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:einleitung}

Instruction-following LLM applications face a fundamental ambiguity:
natural-language input can contain both data to be processed and instructions
that appear actionable. Unlike conventional software interfaces with explicitly
enforced data types and permissions, an LLM determines through learned
behaviour which parts of its context to follow. This becomes a security problem
when trusted instructions and attacker-controlled content are processed
together.

Prompt injection exploits this ambiguity by attempting to redirect the model
away from its trusted task
\parencites{perez2022ignore}{greshake2023indirect}. The injected instruction may
arrive through the user's direct input or through external content retrieved by
the application. The 2025 OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications assigns prompt
injection the identifier \emph{LLM01}, meaning that it is the first risk
category listed in that document
\parencite{owasp2025llm}.

Applications commonly distinguish developer instructions, user requests,
retrieved content, and tool results through roles or delimiters. These
distinctions can influence model behaviour, but they do not provide an
independently enforced authority mechanism. Unlike a prepared SQL statement, a
prompt format does not reliably prevent content intended as data from being
interpreted as an instruction
\parencites{liu2023promptinjection}{wallace2024instruction}.

StruQ and SecAlign address this problem by training models to preserve a
separation between trusted instructions and untrusted data
\parencites{chen2025struq}{chen2025secalign}. Their evaluations, however, use
different models, attacks, optimisation methods, and success criteria. Later
studies additionally test the defences with attack methods adapted to their
training procedures
\parencites{jia2025critical}
             {yang2025checkpointgcg}
             {pandya2025astra}. The resulting evidence must therefore be
interpreted in relation to the attacker model and evaluation scope rather than
as a universal measure of prompt-injection resistance.

This paper examines both the effectiveness of learned instruction--data
separation and the controls required outside the model when that separation
fails.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Research Question} \quad
\textit{How effectively do learned instruction--data separation approaches
mitigate prompt injection under the evaluated attacker models, and what
system-level controls are required when they do not provide an independently
enforced security boundary?}

\medskip
The paper presents a scoped narrative review of StruQ, SecAlign, and subsequent
defence-adaptive evaluations. The studies are compared according to their
attacker assumptions, attack construction methods, success criteria, utility
effects, and enforcement assumptions. No original experiments or independent
replications are conducted.

Chapter~\ref{sec:llms} introduces LLM prompting and structured context.
Chapter~\ref{sec:prompt-injection} describes prompt-injection delivery and
construction methods, while Chapter~\ref{sec:threat} defines the threat model
used to interpret the evidence. Chapter~\ref{sec:struq} examines StruQ and
SecAlign, and Chapter~\ref{sec:limits} discusses the limits of their reported
robustness. Chapter~7 derives practical deployment implications, and
Chapter~\ref{sec:conclusion} answers the research question.
