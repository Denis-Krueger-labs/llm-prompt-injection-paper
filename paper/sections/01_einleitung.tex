\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:einleitung}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have become an integral part of modern software.
They are increasingly embedded in applications that read emails, process
documents, browse websites, and interact with external services. Their ability
to reason over large amounts of data makes them highly useful, but also
introduces new security challenges. As LLMs become more deeply integrated into
automated workflows and agentic systems, the consequences of successful
manipulation extend beyond incorrect answers and may affect entire systems.

The threat this paper focuses on is \textit{prompt injection}. In these
attacks, adversaries embed malicious instructions into content that the model
is asked to process. Because current LLMs do not maintain a strict separation
between instructions and data, injected content may influence the model's
behaviour, manipulate outputs, or expose sensitive
information~\parencite{perez2022ignore, greshake2023indirect}.

The underlying issue is a boundary problem. Human readers naturally distinguish
between instructions and quoted content, whereas LLMs operate purely on textual
patterns. Consequently, any application that processes untrusted external data
is structurally vulnerable to prompt injection
attacks~\parencite{liu2023promptinjection}. The severity of the problem is
reflected by OWASP, which lists prompt injection as the most significant
security risk for LLM-integrated applications~\parencite{owasp2024llm}.

Unlike traditional vulnerabilities such as SQL injection, the distinction
between instructions and data is largely semantic rather than syntactic. LLMs
must interpret user instructions while simultaneously reasoning about external
content within the same context window. As a result, the boundary between
trusted and untrusted information is not formally enforced.

Researchers have proposed a variety of defences, ranging from input filtering
and sanitisation to architectural approaches that attempt to separate
instructions from data. However, recent studies demonstrate that even advanced
defences remain vulnerable to adaptive attacks and that their effectiveness is
often overestimated~\parencite{yang2025checkpointgcg, pandya2025astra,
jia2025critical}. The gap between claimed and actual security guarantees
therefore remains one of the central challenges addressed in this paper.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Research Question} \quad
\textit{How effective is the separation of instructions and data as a defence
strategy against prompt injection, and where do the limits of current LLM
prompt injection defences lie?}

\medskip
To answer this question, the paper first introduces the foundations of LLMs
and prompting before examining prompt injection attacks, threat models,
existing defence mechanisms, their limitations, and practical implications for
secure deployment.
