\section{Limits of Current Defences}
\label{sec:limits}

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology Problems}

A recurring issue in prompt injection defence research is that reported
robustness often does not reflect realistic adversarial conditions.
\textcite{jia2025critical} argue that many evaluations rely on assumptions
that are too narrow to support the conclusions drawn from them.

One common problem is attack diversity. Many studies primarily evaluate
defences against heuristic attacks, such as manually written prompts that
attempt to override system instructions. While these attacks provide a useful
baseline, they do not represent adversaries who actively adapt their strategy
to the defence being tested~\parencite{jia2025critical}.

A second issue concerns prompt diversity. Several defences, including StruQ
and SecAlign in their original evaluations, were tested using only a limited
set of injected prompts. More comprehensive evaluations reveal considerably
higher attack success rates than originally reported, suggesting that
robustness can be overestimated when the range of attacks is too narrow
~\parencite{jia2025critical}.

Finally, utility preservation is difficult to measure consistently. Different
evaluation methodologies can lead to substantially different conclusions about
the practical cost of a defence. As a result, models that appear secure and
utility-preserving under one benchmark may perform considerably worse under
more rigorous evaluation settings~\parencite{jia2025critical}.

\subsection{Adaptive Attacks}

The clearest evidence that current defences are less robust than initially
reported comes from adaptive attacks. Unlike standard attacks, adaptive
attacks are specifically designed to target the defence mechanism itself.

\textcite{yang2025checkpointgcg} introduce Checkpoint-GCG, an attack that
exploits intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints to construct adversarial suffixes
that transfer to the final defended model. Their results demonstrate that
SecAlign's apparent robustness against standard GCG does not imply that the
underlying vulnerability has been eliminated. Instead, the attack simply
exploits weaknesses that remain hidden under conventional evaluation
conditions~\parencite{yang2025checkpointgcg}.

A related approach is proposed by \textcite{pandya2025astra}. Their ASTRA
attack targets the model's attention mechanism rather than output token
probabilities. Experimental results show that ASTRA and its universal variant
ASTRA++ achieve high success rates even under weak-knowledge assumptions,
demonstrating that fine-tuning alone cannot reliably prevent prompt injection
attacks~\parencite{pandya2025astra}.

These results also highlight an important limitation of GCG as an evaluation
tool. According to \textcite{pandya2025astra}, gradient-based optimisation
provides only a weak signal against defended models. Consequently, poor
performance of standard GCG should not necessarily be interpreted as evidence
of strong robustness, but may instead reflect the limitations of the attack
itself.

\subsection{Security and Utility Trade-Offs}

Improved robustness does not come without cost. Every defence mechanism
discussed in this paper introduces trade-offs between security and utility.

Detection-based approaches may reduce attack success rates, but often suffer
from false positives that negatively affect benign tasks
~\parencite{debenedetti2024agentdojo, jia2025critical}. Fine-tuning-based
defences such as StruQ and SecAlign also impose measurable capability losses
when evaluated against stronger baselines~\parencite{jia2025critical}.

More generally, robustness and instruction-following capability are inherently
in tension. A model trained to distrust instructions contained within external
data risks becoming overly cautious and incorrectly treating legitimate content
as malicious. While approaches such as SecAlign alleviate this problem to some
extent, the trade-off cannot be eliminated entirely
~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{Why Prompt Injection Remains an Open Problem}

The evidence reviewed in this section points to a consistent pattern.
Defences that appear robust under standard evaluation conditions often weaken
significantly when confronted with stronger attackers or more rigorous testing
methodologies~\parencite{jia2025critical, yang2025checkpointgcg}.

The deeper issue is structural. Prompt injection exploits one of the defining
strengths of LLMs --- their ability to follow natural language instructions.
As long as instructions and external data are processed within the same context
window, the attack surface remains. Fine-tuning can increase the cost of a
successful attack, but it cannot eliminate the underlying condition.

Consequently, no currently available defence provides complete protection
against prompt injection. Rather than viewing prompt injection as an isolated
model weakness, it should be understood as a broader system-level problem.
This perspective motivates the practical deployment considerations discussed in
the following section.
