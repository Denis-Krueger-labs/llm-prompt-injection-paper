\section{Limits of the Case-Study Defences}
\label{sec:limits}

The original StruQ and SecAlign evaluations show that learned
instruction--data separation can reduce prompt-injection success. Their results
remain conditional on the selected models, tasks, attacks, attacker access,
optimisation budgets, and success criteria. This section examines how the
evidence changes under broader and defence-adaptive evaluations.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology Problems}
\label{subsec:evaluation-methodology}

Attack Success Rate (ASR) is meaningful only when the counted success event is
defined. StruQ manually checks responses containing \enquote{Hacked!}, whereas
SecAlign counts an attack as successful when the response begins with
\enquote{Hacked} or \enquote{hacked}
\parencites{chen2025struq}{chen2025secalign}. Such target-output criteria provide
repeatable proxies for instruction-following compromise, but they do not
directly measure confidential-data disclosure, unauthorised tool execution, or
open-ended attacker objectives
\parencite{jia2025critical}.

Results from different studies are therefore not directly rankable. Changes in
models, datasets, prompts, attacker knowledge, optimisation methods, and success
rules can substantially alter the reported outcome.

Jia et al. evaluate OpenPromptInjection and MMLU-PI, a prompt-injection
benchmark derived from the Massive Multitask Language Understanding benchmark.
They use a task-specific Attack Success Value (ASV), which measures how
successfully the model performs the injected task. Higher values indicate a
more successful attack and therefore a less effective defence. Because ASV is
based on task-specific performance rather than only a binary target-string
criterion, it is not directly equivalent to ASR
\parencite{jia2025critical}.

Under adaptive GCG, StruQ reached an ASV of \(1.00\) on both benchmarks, meaning
that the attack reached the maximum value of the evaluation metric. SecAlign
reached \(0.46\) on OpenPromptInjection and \(0.88\) on MMLU-PI
\parencite{jia2025critical}.

This broader evaluation suggests that the strong results of earlier studies
were partly enabled by narrow test settings, including limited task diversity,
fixed target outputs, and attacks that were not fully adapted to the defence.
Robustness observed within such a test scope should therefore not be
generalised to stronger attacker models
\parencite{jia2025critical}.

The same caution applies to utility. SecAlign reports AlpacaEval2 results
relative to a \texttt{davinci003} reference, whereas Jia et al. compare the
released defended variants with the corresponding undefended Llama model and
additional ground-truth tasks
\parencites{chen2025secalign}{jia2025critical}. Their lower utility scores
therefore demonstrate a trade-off in that evaluation setting rather than a
direct contradiction of the original results.

\subsection{Adaptive Attacks}
\label{subsec:adaptive-attacks}

Adaptive attacks target assumptions or training procedures specific to the
defence. They are useful for testing worst-case robustness, although their
access requirements may be less representative of attacks against closed
services.

Checkpoint-GCG attacks successive intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints. A
suffix found against one checkpoint is used to initialise the attack against
the next. With access to the full model input and checkpoints, the method
achieved ASRs of \(88\%\), \(96\%\), and \(84\%\) against SecAlign-defended
Llama, Mistral, and Qwen models. Universal suffixes reached \(75.3\%\),
\(89.9\%\), and \(78.3\%\) on held-out prompts, while one Llama-derived suffix
transferred to a related model with \(63.9\%\) ASR
\parencite{yang2025checkpointgcg}.

These results show that resistance to ordinary GCG does not imply resistance to
checkpoint-aware optimisation. However, the attack assumes unusually strong
access to the model, its full input, and intermediate training states.

ASTRA uses attention-guided white-box optimisation followed by a GCG-style
target objective. Its token budget specifies how many adversarial tokens the
attacker may optimise and add to the payload. A larger budget gives the attack
more opportunities to construct an effective suffix, but also produces a
longer and potentially less inconspicuous payload.

At a budget of 35 adversarial tokens, ASTRA achieved \(72.5\%\) ASR against
SecAlign-defended Llama and \(82.5\%\) against Mistral
\parencite{pandya2025astra}. These results demonstrate bypasses under the tested
white-box conditions, but do not establish the same success against closed APIs
or deployments with additional application-level controls.

Together, the adaptive evaluations show that StruQ and SecAlign do not create a
deterministic or independently enforced trust boundary. They do not establish,
however, that every model-level defence must fail under every deployment
condition.

\subsection{Security and Utility Trade-Offs}
\label{subsec:security-utility}

A useful defence must reduce malicious instruction following without preventing
legitimate analysis of instructional text. A system that rejects every
imperative sentence may achieve a low ASR while becoming unusable, whereas a
model that preserves broad instruction-following behaviour may retain a larger
attack surface.

Security and utility should therefore be reported together under clearly
specified models, tasks, references, attack budgets, and judging methods
\parencites{chen2025struq}{chen2025secalign}{jia2025critical}.

\subsection{Limitations of This Review}
\label{subsec:review-limitations}

This paper is a selective narrative review and contains no original experiments
or independent replications. It focuses on StruQ, SecAlign, and later attacks
against those defences. Comparisons remain limited by differences in models,
prompts, tasks, attacker access, optimisation budgets, and success definitions.

Some adaptive evidence is available only as preprint research, while closed
providers may not disclose enough information for reproducible evaluation. The
additional exclusions defined in Section~\ref{subsec:threat-scope} also apply
to this review.

\subsection{Implications of the Evidence}
\label{subsec:open-problem}

Learned instruction--data separation substantially reduces the handcrafted
attacks represented in the original evaluations, and SecAlign improves
resistance to the optimisation-based attacks tested in its study. Broader and
defence-adaptive evaluations nevertheless show that this robustness is
conditional on the evaluation scope and does not constitute an independently
enforced guarantee.

Instruction--data separation should therefore be treated as risk reduction
rather than complete prevention. Deployment must assume that model-level
protection can fail and constrain resulting information flows and actions
through controls outside the model.
