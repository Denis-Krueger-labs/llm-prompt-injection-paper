\section{Limits of the Case-Study Defences}
\label{sec:limits}

The original StruQ and SecAlign evaluations show that learned
instruction--data separation can reduce prompt-injection success. Their results
remain conditional, however, on the selected models, tasks, attacks, attacker
access, optimisation budgets, and success criteria. This section therefore
examines how the evidence changes under broader and defence-adaptive
evaluations.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology Problems}
\label{subsec:evaluation-methodology}

Attack Success Rate (ASR) is meaningful only when the counted success event is
defined. StruQ manually checks responses containing \enquote{Hacked!}, whereas
SecAlign counts a response as successful when it begins with
\enquote{Hacked} or \enquote{hacked}
\parencites{chen2025struq}{chen2025secalign}. Such target-output criteria provide
repeatable proxies for instruction-following compromise, but they do not
directly measure confidential-data disclosure, unauthorised tool execution, or
open-ended attacker objectives
\parencite{jia2025critical}.

Results from different studies are therefore not directly rankable. Changes in
model versions, datasets, prompts, attacker knowledge, optimisation methods, and
success rules can substantially alter the reported outcome. The later
evaluation by Jia et al., for example, uses OpenPromptInjection and MMLU-PI,
different utility references, and a task-specific Attack Success Value (ASV).
Under adaptive GCG, StruQ reached an ASV of \(1.00\) on both benchmarks, while
SecAlign reached \(0.46\) on OpenPromptInjection and \(0.88\) on MMLU-PI
\parencite{jia2025critical}.

The same caution applies to utility. SecAlign reports AlpacaEval2 results
relative to a \texttt{davinci003} reference, whereas Jia et al. compare released
defended variants with the corresponding undefended Llama model and additional
ground-truth tasks
\parencites{chen2025secalign}{jia2025critical}. Their lower utility scores
therefore show a trade-off in that setting, not a contradiction of the original
evaluation.

\subsection{Adaptive Attacks}
\label{subsec:adaptive-attacks}

Adaptive attacks target assumptions or training procedures specific to the
defence. They are useful for testing worst-case robustness, although their
access requirements may be less representative of attacks against closed
services.

Checkpoint-GCG attacks successive intermediate fine-tuning checkpoints. A
suffix found against one checkpoint is used to initialise the attack against
the next. With access to the full model input and checkpoints, the method
achieved ASRs of \(88\%\), \(96\%\), and \(84\%\) against SecAlign-defended
Llama, Mistral, and Qwen models. Universal suffixes reached \(75.3\%\),
\(89.9\%\), and \(78.3\%\) on held-out prompts, while one Llama-derived suffix
transferred to a related model with \(63.9\%\) ASR
\parencite{yang2025checkpointgcg}. These results show that resistance to
ordinary GCG does not imply resistance to checkpoint-aware optimisation, but
the attack assumes unusually strong audit access.

ASTRA instead uses attention-guided white-box optimisation followed by a
GCG-style target objective. At a 35-token budget, it achieved \(72.5\%\) ASR
against SecAlign-defended Llama and \(82.5\%\) against Mistral. ASTRA++, which
withholds the preceding context while retaining model and defence access,
reached \(96\%\) against SecAlign and \(87\%\) against SecAlign++
\parencite{pandya2025astra}. These results demonstrate bypasses under the tested
white-box conditions, but do not establish the same success against closed APIs
or deployments with additional application-level controls.

Together, the adaptive evaluations show that StruQ and SecAlign do not create a
deterministic or independently enforced trust boundary. They do not show that
every model-level defence must fail under every deployment condition.

\subsection{Security and Utility Trade-Offs}
\label{subsec:security-utility}

A useful defence must reduce malicious instruction following without preventing
legitimate analysis of instructional text. A system that rejects every
imperative sentence may achieve a low ASR while becoming unusable, whereas a
model that preserves broad instruction-following behaviour may retain a larger
attack surface. Security results should therefore be reported together with
utility under clearly specified models, tasks, references, and judging methods
\parencites{chen2025struq}{chen2025secalign}{jia2025critical}.

\subsection{Limitations of This Review}
\label{subsec:review-limitations}

This paper is a selective narrative review and contains no original experiments
or independent replications. It focuses on text-based prompt injection, StruQ,
SecAlign, and later attacks against those defences. Comparisons are limited by
differences in models, prompts, tasks, attacker access, optimisation budgets,
and success definitions. Some adaptive evidence remains available only as
preprint research, while closed providers may not disclose enough information
for reproducible evaluation. Multimodal injection, persistent memory attacks,
denial of service, insiders, provider compromise, and training-data poisoning
are outside the scope of this review.

\subsection{Implications of the Evidence}
\label{subsec:open-problem}

The evidence supports three conclusions. First, learned instruction--data
separation substantially reduces the handcrafted attacks represented in the
original StruQ and SecAlign evaluations. Second, SecAlign improves resistance
to the optimisation-based attacks included in its own evaluation. Third, later
defence-adaptive white-box attacks demonstrate that this robustness is not an
independently enforced guarantee.

Separating instructions and data should therefore be treated as risk reduction,
not complete prevention. Model-level robustness remains useful, but deployment
must assume that it can fail and constrain resulting information flows and
actions through controls outside the model.
