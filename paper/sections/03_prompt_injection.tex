\section{Prompt Injection Attacks}
\label{sec:attacks}

As discussed in the previous section, the flexibility that makes LLMs useful
also creates new attack surfaces. Prompt injection exploits this property by
embedding malicious instructions into content processed by the model. The goal
is to influence the model's behaviour and cause it to follow attacker-controlled
instructions instead of the intended ones~\parencite{perez2022ignore}.

Unlike traditional vulnerabilities, prompt injection does not exploit flaws in
software implementation. Instead, it takes advantage of the fact that current
LLMs process instructions and external content within the same context window,
without any explicit mechanism for separating trusted instructions from
untrusted data~\parencite{chen2025struq}. As a result, attackers can exploit
this ambiguity to manipulate model behaviour. OWASP therefore lists prompt
injection as the most significant security risk for LLM-integrated
applications~\parencite{owasp2024llm}.

\subsection{Direct and Indirect Prompt Injection}

The literature distinguishes between two primary attack variants, depending on
how malicious instructions are delivered~\parencite{greshake2023indirect}.

\textit{Direct prompt injection} occurs when attackers interact with the model
directly and attempt to override system instructions through specially crafted
inputs. Typical examples include prompts such as ``ignore previous
instructions'' or other attempts to bypass safety mechanisms
~\parencite{perez2022ignore}.

\textit{Indirect prompt injection} is generally more dangerous in practical
deployments. Here, malicious instructions are hidden inside external content
such as emails, websites, documents, or API responses. Since retrieval-augmented
generation systems and LLM agents continuously process such data, users may be
unaware that an attack is taking place~\parencite{greshake2023indirect}. As
shown by \textcite{debenedetti2024agentdojo}, untrusted tool outputs represent
an effective attack vector in agentic systems.

\subsection{Why Prompt Injection Works and Its Consequences}

The root cause of prompt injection lies in the architecture of current language
models. LLMs operate on sequences of tokens and have no internal notion of
provenance. Consequently, they cannot reliably distinguish between trusted
system instructions and untrusted content supplied by external
sources~\parencite{liu2023promptinjection}. This problem is further amplified
by the fact that modern LLMs are explicitly optimised to follow instructions,
making them highly useful but also inherently susceptible to manipulation.

The consequences of successful prompt injection depend on the capabilities of
the affected system. \textcite{fspib2025} distinguish between
\textit{information-level} and \textit{action-level} attacks. At the
information level, attackers may manipulate outputs, extract sensitive
information, or reveal hidden system prompts. In agentic systems, prompt
injection can additionally influence tool calls and interactions with external
services, potentially causing unintended actions on behalf of the
user~\parencite{fspib2025}.

The attack surface is not limited to the LLM itself. Prompt injection can
propagate into connected systems and may therefore serve as a bridge between
natural language manipulation and more traditional attack vectors
~\parencite{pedro2025p2sql}. Real-world incidents, such as attacks against
Slack AI, demonstrate that these risks are not merely theoretical
~\parencite{chen2025secalign}.

\subsection{Attack Taxonomy}

Prompt injection attacks can be further classified according to the method used
to construct the injected content.

\textit{Heuristic attacks} rely on manually crafted instructions designed to
override the original task. Examples include Context Ignoring attacks,
Fake Completion attacks, and combinations of multiple techniques
~\parencite{jia2025critical}. These attacks are relatively simple to construct
and remain effective against many systems.

\textit{Optimisation-based attacks} employ gradient-guided search techniques to
generate adversarial token sequences that maximise the probability of achieving
a desired outcome. The Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) algorithm introduced by
\textcite{zou2023universal} represents one of the most prominent approaches.
Compared to heuristic attacks, optimisation-based methods are considerably more
difficult to defend against and play an important role in evaluating the
limitations of current defence mechanisms discussed later in this paper.
