\section{LLMs and Prompting}
\label{sec:llms}

Before discussing the security implications of prompt injection attacks and the
mechanisms proposed to mitigate them, it is necessary to understand the
underlying technology on which these attacks operate.

Large Language Models (LLMs) are a class of artificial intelligence systems
trained on large amounts of textual data. Their primary objective is to predict
the next token in a sequence, enabling them to generate coherent and
contextually relevant responses. Through this process, modern LLMs are capable
of performing a wide variety of tasks, including summarisation, translation,
question answering, and code generation.

Interaction with an LLM occurs through prompts. In practical deployments, the
model typically receives both developer-defined instructions and user-supplied
input. The developer-defined instructions, commonly referred to as the
\textit{system prompt}, specify the desired behaviour of the model, while the
\textit{user prompt} contains the task or request supplied by the user. These
inputs are combined into a single context window and processed together by the
model.

This architecture represents the foundation of prompt injection attacks. Since
instructions and user input are processed within the same token sequence, the
boundary between trusted instructions and untrusted content is not enforced
structurally. Consequently, an attacker may attempt to craft inputs that
override or interfere with the intended behaviour of the system
~\parencite{liu2023promptinjection}.

This absence of a structural boundary between trusted instructions and untrusted
data is the fundamental condition that makes prompt injection attacks possible,
as the following section examines in detail.
